The Unconventional Path

Why I Do This Work

I've spent my career at the intersection of education and engagement—figuring out how to reach students who've been labeled "difficult" or "unmotivated" by systems that weren't designed for their brains.

My background in Special Education taught me the science: how executive function develops, why some brains struggle with planning and impulse control, and what actually helps. But it also showed me what doesn't work—namely, more worksheets, more lectures, more "try harder."

My training in improv and comedy taught me the art: how to connect with anyone, how to make a room feel safe for failure, and how to turn frustration into play. When you're doing improv, you can't be in your head. You have to respond, adapt, and keep going. That's exactly what executive function is.

The "Aha" Moment

I discovered the power of games when I started using chess with a student who refused to engage with anything academic. He was bright—everyone knew it—but homework felt like torture.

Chess was different. It had stakes. It was competitive. And critically, every loss was immediate feedback on "I moved too fast" or "I didn't think ahead." We could talk about his impulsivity without it feeling like a lecture.

Within months, he started applying the same "stop and think" approach to his schoolwork. Not because I told him to—because he'd discovered, through the game, that it worked.

That's when I realized: the game isn't a reward for doing the "real work." The game is the work.

My Philosophy

01

Engagement First

If a student isn't engaged, nothing else matters. I'd rather play chess and make incremental progress than do worksheets and create resentment. Buy-in is the foundation of change.

02

Skills Over Content

I don't teach algebra. I teach the executive function skills that let a student tackle algebra on their own—planning, starting, persisting, and recovering from mistakes.

03

Progress Over Perfection

Executive function develops slowly. I celebrate small wins and focus on trends, not single data points. A bad week isn't failure—it's information.

04

Respect the Student

Neurodivergent students have been talked at, lectured, and "fixed" their whole lives. I approach them as collaborators. Their insights about their own brains matter.

05

Fun Is Functional

Play isn't a reward—it's a mechanism. Games create the conditions for learning: low stakes, immediate feedback, intrinsic motivation. That's not soft; that's science.

06

Parents Are Partners

I keep parents informed, but I don't spy on students. The coaching relationship requires trust. I'll share progress and concerns—but not every detail of every conversation.

What I'm Not

Clarity about scope is important. Here's what's outside my practice.

Not a Therapist

I don't provide mental health therapy, diagnose conditions, or treat clinical anxiety/depression. I'll refer out to psychologists when appropriate.

Not a Tutor

I don't teach subject content (math, science, writing). I teach the executive function skills that help students learn content on their own.

Not a Lawyer

For IEP work, I provide translation and support—not legal advocacy. For disputes requiring legal action, I'll recommend special education attorneys.

Not a Quick Fix

Executive function skills build over months and years, not sessions. I won't promise overnight transformation—only consistent progress.

A Personal Note

I got into this work because I've seen what happens when bright kids fall through the cracks—not because they lack ability, but because the system wasn't built for their wiring.

The students I work with aren't "broken." They're not "lazy." They have brains that work differently, and they need strategies and skills that most schools don't teach.

My job is to bridge that gap—to teach the executive function skills that let smart kids actually show what they know. And I've found that the best way to teach those skills isn't through lectures or worksheets. It's through play.

If that resonates, I'd love to talk.

— Jacob Rozansky
