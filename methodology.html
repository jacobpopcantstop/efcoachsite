The Play-Based Neuroplasticity Method

How I use Improv, Chess, and Speed Cubing to train the executive function skills that traditional tutoring can't touch.

What is Executive Function?

Executive Function (EF) is your brain's "CEO"—the set of mental skills that let you plan, focus, remember instructions, and juggle multiple tasks. It's not about how smart you are. It's about how you manage what you know.

💡

Working Memory

Holding information in mind while using it. Like keeping the instructions in your head while you're building something.

🚫

Inhibition

The "stop and think" brake. Pausing before acting, resisting impulses, and filtering out distractions.

🔄

Cognitive Flexibility

Shifting gears when plans change. Adapting to new information instead of getting stuck.

📅

Planning & Prioritization

Breaking big goals into steps, figuring out what's most important, and creating a path forward.

⏱

Time Management

Understanding how long things take, meeting deadlines, and not losing track of time.

🎯

Task Initiation

Starting tasks without excessive procrastination. Getting going even when motivation is low.

Framework #1

The "Yes, And..." Flexibility Framework

Tool: Improv Games

The Science

Research shows that improv training significantly lowers social anxiety and improves divergent thinking in neurodivergent populations. The "Yes, And" principle—accepting what's given and building on it—directly trains cognitive flexibility.

What It Trains

  • Cognitive Flexibility: Plans change every second in improv. You can't script it. You learn to adapt.
  • Impulse Control: Good improv requires listening before responding. You can't just blurt out your idea.
  • Active Listening: You must track what others say to build on it. Zoning out means you miss your cue.
  • Frustration Tolerance: Scenes fail. Ideas flop. You learn to laugh and try again.

Example Exercises

"One Word Story"

Two people build a story one word at a time. Forces you to let go of your plan and respond to what actually happened.

"New Choice"

A facilitator yells "new choice" and you must instantly change what you just said or did. Trains rapid mental pivoting.

"Emotional Rollercoaster"

Change emotional states on command mid-scene. Builds awareness and control of emotional regulation.

Framework #2

The "Checkmate" Focus Protocol

Tool: Chess

The Science

Chess requires "stop and think" mechanics that directly counteract the impulsivity found in ADHD. It forces the externalization of executive function—you must visualize future moves, weigh consequences, and inhibit the temptation to play the first move you see.

What It Trains

  • Inhibition: The urge to play fast is strong. Chess punishes it. You learn to pause.
  • Planning: You can't win without thinking ahead. "If I do this, then they'll do that, then I'll..."
  • Consequence Thinking: Every move has ripple effects. Chess makes abstract consequences concrete.
  • Sustained Attention: A game requires 20-60 minutes of focus. It's attention training with stakes.

How I'm Different From a Chess Coach

I'm not teaching you the Sicilian Defense or aiming for a high rating. I use chess as a biofeedback device for attention. We discuss the moments where impulse won over strategy, and build metacognitive awareness about thinking patterns.

A Typical Chess Moment

Student: Moves knight without thinking.

Me: "What did you notice your brain doing before that move?"

Student: "I just... moved."

Me: "Cool. Let's rewind. What would 'stop, survey, decide' look like here?"

Framework #3

The "Algorithm" Approach

Tool: Speed Cubing (Rubik's Cube)

The Science

Procedural learning—memorizing and executing sequences of steps—strengthens working memory and builds frustration tolerance. The cube also demonstrates that "impossible" tasks are just undiscovered algorithms.

What It Trains

  • Working Memory: Algorithms are 6-12 moves long. You must hold them in mind while executing.
  • Procedural Learning: The cube teaches that complex skills are just memorized sequences. Essays, projects, morning routines—all algorithms.
  • Frustration Tolerance: You will mess up. The cube scrambles. You start over. You learn to handle setbacks.
  • Growth Mindset: Every kid thinks the cube is "impossible" at first. Then they solve it. That feeling transfers.

The Bigger Lesson

When a student learns to solve a Rubik's cube, they prove to themselves that "I can't do it" is often "I don't know the steps yet." This mindset shift applies to:

  • Writing an essay (algorithm: brainstorm → outline → draft → revise)
  • Studying for a test (algorithm: review notes → practice problems → self-quiz)
  • Managing a project (algorithm: list tasks → order by priority → work in blocks)

The Research Behind the Method

This isn't just my opinion. Play-based approaches to executive function are supported by cognitive science.

On Improv & Cognitive Flexibility

Studies show improvisation training improves divergent thinking, reduces anxiety around uncertainty, and builds tolerance for ambiguity—all critical for students who struggle with rigidity.

On Chess & Inhibitory Control

Chess requires players to suppress impulsive responses and consider consequences before acting. Research shows regular chess practice correlates with improved self-regulation in children.

On Procedural Learning & Working Memory

Learning algorithms (like cube solutions) exercises working memory and demonstrates that complex skills are achievable through structured practice—building self-efficacy.

On Engagement & Learning

The neuroscience is clear: learning sticks better when the learner is engaged and enjoying the process. Dopamine isn't just "fun"—it's a learning signal.
